You Were Built to Work.
Nobody Asked If That's
What You Wanted.

How the Indian education system was designed to produce workers — and why most of us never noticed.

Here is something they never told you in school.

In 1835, a British politician named Thomas Babington Macaulay wrote a document that would quietly shape the minds of every Indian child for the next two centuries. He called it the Minute on Education. In it, he argued that Indian knowledge and literature were worthless, and that what India needed was — in his own words — a class of persons Indian in blood and colour, but English in tastes, in opinions, in morals and in intellect.

Not thinkers. Not creators. Not people who question things.

Intermediaries. Administrators. People who could sit at a desk, follow instructions, and keep the colonial machine running without needing too much supervision.

That was the blueprint. And almost everything built after it followed that blueprint — including the system you and I grew up inside.

Before They Came

Before British rule, India had its own systems of learning. Gurukuls, pathshalas, madrasas — decentralised, community-rooted, and focused on the whole person. Philosophy, mathematics, astronomy, medicine, trade, the arts. A child learned what their community needed and what their mind was drawn to.

It wasn't perfect. Nothing is. But it was alive. It was connected to real life.

Then came the East India Company. And with it, a very different idea of what education was for.

Historical record British historian Krishan Kumar described colonial education spending as "investment in preparation of trustworthy, cheap subordinates." Francis Warden, director of the East India Company during 1836–1850, stated that education would only be "beneficial" through "judicious encouragement" of higher sections of Indian society — meaning: educate just enough people to serve us, and keep it controlled.

The 1835 English Education Act eliminated indigenous knowledge from the curriculum entirely. Sanskrit, Persian, classical sciences — gone. Replaced by English literature, British history told from a British perspective, and economics framed around capitalism and materialism. Not because these things were more valuable. But because they produced the kind of person the Empire needed.

A clerk. An administrator. A worker who knew enough to be useful, but not enough to be dangerous.

Independence Changed the Flag. Not the System.

In 1947, India became free. The British left. The education system stayed.

Think about that for a moment. We changed the government, the currency, the leadership — but the machine that shaped every child's mind was left largely intact. The same structure. The same emphasis on memorisation over thinking. The same hierarchy where passing an exam means more than understanding anything. The same finish line: get a degree, get a job, be stable.

Nobody asked why we were still running a system designed by colonisers to serve colonisers.

What it costs today ₹18–35 lakh Average fees for a 2-year MBA at an IIM. Engineering degrees at premier private institutions cost ₹16–40 lakh for four years. A significant portion of Indian families take on debt they spend years repaying — for a paper that increasingly struggles to guarantee the stability it promises.

We pay more for it now. It is dressed up more expensively. But the core design is the same: sit still, absorb what you're told, prove it on a paper, and you will be rewarded with employment.

Education became a transaction. Pay for the degree. Get the job. Validate your worth to society.

The Paper and What It Actually Proves

Here is what a degree proves, if we're being completely honest.

It proves you could stay inside a system for three or four years without leaving. It proves you could absorb information in a format someone else designed, reproduce it under pressure, and score well enough to pass. These are real skills. Endurance. Compliance. Pattern recognition. They are useful in certain environments.

What it does not prove: that you can think independently. That you can solve a problem nobody has solved before. That you know what you want, or who you are, or what you're actually capable of when nobody is grading you.

And yet we treat it as the measure of a person. We use it to decide who gets the interview, who gets the loan, who gets taken seriously at the dinner table.

The people who built the tools Ritesh Agarwal dropped out of college at 17 and built OYO Rooms into a company valued at billions. Bhavin Turakhia dropped out after school at 18 and built Directi into a global technology company with millions of customers. Kailash Katkar failed ninth standard and dropped out — then built Quick Heal, India's first antivirus software, into a ₹200 crore business. Kunal Shah dropped out of his MBA and built FreeCharge and CRED. These are not exceptions that prove the rule. They are evidence that the rule was always wrong.

The people who built the technology that runs our world — the platforms, the software, the infrastructure — many of them left the system early. Not because they were failures. Because they saw something the system couldn't teach them, and they went to find it themselves.

The Social Pressure Nobody Talks About Honestly

I want to say something that most people think but few write down clearly.

The reason most people don't leave the system — even when they know something is wrong — is not laziness. It is fear. And that fear is manufactured.

We are told from childhood that there is one path. Study hard. Get into a good college. Get a stable job. That is safety. That is success. That is what responsible people do. And anyone who questions it is called naive, risky, impractical, or privileged.

I have had business ideas that felt real, felt right, felt like something worth building. And I watched them fade — not because they were bad ideas, but because I didn't know how to execute them, and because everyone around me was quietly saying: that's too uncertain. Get the job first. Have a backup.

The backup became the plan. The plan became the cage. And the cage was called security.

The equation nobody teaches Skill + Trust = The ability to earn. Not degree + compliance = employment. Skill is what you can genuinely do. Trust is what people believe about your ability to do it. Both can be built without a classroom. Neither is guaranteed by a certificate.

What Learning Actually Is

Here is what I have come to believe, after watching myself and the people around me navigate this.

Learning is not linear. It is not a staircase where you complete one step before moving to the next. It is messy, non-sequential, personal, and often accidental. The most important things I have learned did not come from lectures. They came from conversations, failures, books I read out of curiosity, problems I needed to solve, questions I couldn't stop asking.

Everything connects to everything else. Finance connects to psychology. History connects to design. Biology connects to decision-making. When you find the thing you genuinely care about and pull on that thread, every subject eventually shows up. You don't need to be taught subjects in isolation. You need to care about something real, and then watch how the world reveals itself through that lens.

And you do not need a degree to earn. You need skill — the genuine ability to do something useful — and trust — the credibility that makes people willing to pay you for it. Both of these take time to build. Neither of them requires permission from an institution.

This Is Not a Rant Against Education

I want to be clear about something before I finish.

I am not saying don't go to college. I am not saying degrees are useless or that dropping out is brave. I am saying something more specific than that.

I am saying: know why you are doing what you are doing. Know whose idea of success you are chasing. Know what you actually want, beneath what you were told to want. Ask the questions that the system was never designed to help you ask.

Because the system was not designed for you. It was designed for a purpose that had nothing to do with you. And if you go through it without ever stopping to question it, you will arrive at the end of it shaped by forces you never chose and serving a vision of life you never consciously agreed to.

That is the thing worth questioning. Not the teachers, not the institutions, not any individual person inside the system. The design. The assumption underneath all of it that your purpose is to be productive, employable, and economically useful — and that everything else is secondary.

"What if the most important question education never asked you is the same one you've never asked yourself: what kind of life do you actually want to live?"

I don't have the answer to that for you. Nobody does.

But I think it starts with knowing that the question exists. And that you are allowed to ask it. And that whatever answer you find — whatever turns out to be true for you — is more valuable than any grade, any degree, any title anyone else might hand you.

You were built to work.

But you were born to live.

And there is a difference.
